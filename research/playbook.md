## What This Playbook Is — And What It Isn't

This is not a summary of what the experts said.
It is a synthesis of what I believe works, grounded in their
evidence, filtered through my own judgment, and built specifically
for a B2B SaaS company in the recruitment automation space.

Every recommendation is sourced. Every opinion is mine.

---

## The Core Argument

Most B2B SaaS companies treat LinkedIn like a broadcasting
channel. They post product updates, company news, and
generic industry tips. Nobody engages. Nobody buys.

The practitioners who actually generate pipeline on LinkedIn
share one thing in common: they treat the platform as a
**trust-building system**, not a megaphone.

For 100Hires, this distinction is especially important.
HR managers and founders — the core ICP — are among the
most ad-fatigued professionals on LinkedIn. They've seen
every "10 tips to hire faster" post. They ignore them all.

The opportunity is to show up differently: with original
data, specific outcomes, and a genuine point of view on
how hiring is changing. Not to sell. To become the most
credible voice in the room before anyone asks for a demo.

## Section 1 — The Playbook

### 1.1 Build Around One Narrative, Not Many Topics

Pick one specific tension in your market and own it.
Don't post about "HR trends." Post about one specific
shift — and return to it from every angle.

For 100Hires, the natural narrative is:
**"Hiring is broken because it's still manual. It doesn't
have to be."**

Every piece of content should connect back to this tension.
The post formats change. The argument stays the same.

> Source: Katelyn Bourgoin, April 21, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/katebour_some-people-sell-services-some-sell-physical-share-7452326653986533376-HJ7e
> "I sell mental real estate. I help you become known for
> an idea you own in your target buyer's mind. Get that
> right? And selling anything gets easier."

---

### 1.2 Post 2-3 Times Per Week — Never Daily

Consistency matters more than frequency. The accounts
that burned out in 2024-2025 were the ones chasing daily
output. The ones that built durable audiences posted less
but never stopped.

2-3 posts per week, every week, for 12 months, will
outperform daily posting that stops after 6 weeks.

> Source: Richard van der Blom, Algorithm Insights Report 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richardvanderblom_our-annual-algorithm-report-2025-coming-activity-7308091046624702464-WmxY
> Based on analysis of 1.8M posts across 58,000 profiles:
> posting 1-3 times per week is the current optimal cadence.
> Over-posting leads to diminishing returns unless content
> quality is exceptional.

---

### 1.3 Lead With Original Data Whenever Possible

The highest-trust content format on LinkedIn is data
nobody else has. You don't need a research team — you
need a system for turning your operational work into
public insights.

For 100Hires, this means:
- Time-to-hire benchmarks from your customer base
- Screening hours saved per role, per industry
- Response rate comparisons: manual vs. automated outreach
- Drop-off rates at each hiring stage across SMB segments

One data post per month, built from real customer data,
will do more for your pipeline than 30 generic tips posts.

> Source: Oren Greenberg, April 16, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/orengreenberg_i-tracked-101-hyper-growth-companies-activity-7450863592847011841
> "I tracked 101 hyper-growth companies this week to see
> who they're actually hiring. 387 Marketing roles. 224
> Sales roles." — Original research conducted independently
> and published as a LinkedIn post. The data is the content.

> Source: Liam Dunne, April 21, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/liamdunne05_sorry-but-seo-takes-6-months-is-a-cope-activity-7452329063030874113-4Isy
> "Our Series A client increased organic MQLs by 76% in
> 4 weeks from first content published." — Specific client
> outcomes as the primary trust signal.

### 1.4 Structure Posts for Mobile Scanning

72% of LinkedIn activity happens on mobile. Posts that
win are built for a 7-second scan — short paragraphs,
clear line breaks, no walls of text.

The optimal structure:
- **Line 1:** A single sentence that creates tension or
  curiosity. This is your entire distribution budget.
- **Body:** 3-5 short paragraphs or a numbered list.
  Max 150 words.
- **Close:** One question or a soft CTA. Never both.

> Source: Richard van der Blom, Algorithm Insights Report 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richardvanderblom_our-annual-algorithm-report-2025-coming-activity-7308091046624702464-WmxY
> Posts with 800-1,000 characters perform best. Well-formatted
> posts perform 2.1x better due to increased dwell time.
> 72% of LinkedIn traffic comes from mobile devices.

---

### 1.5 Engage For 15-30 Minutes Before And After Posting

This is the most underused tactic on this list and the
most directly supported by data. Commenting on 5-10
relevant posts in the 30 minutes before and after you
publish increases your own post's reach by up to 20%.

It also signals to LinkedIn's algorithm that you are an
active participant, not just a broadcaster.

> Source: Richard van der Blom, Algorithm Insights 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richardvanderblom_our-annual-algorithm-report-2025-coming-activity-7308091046624702464-WmxY
> "Engaging with at least 5 other posts after publishing
> increases visibility and signals to LinkedIn that you're
> active on the platform — not just someone who posts
> and disappears."

---

### 1.6 Publish Frameworks, Not Opinions

On LinkedIn, opinions are cheap. Frameworks are scarce.

An opinion: "Hiring processes are too slow."
A framework: "The 3 stages where SMB hiring breaks down
— and what to automate at each one."

Frameworks get saved. Saves are the highest-value
engagement signal in LinkedIn's 2025 algorithm. A post
with 20 saves and 10 comments will outperform a post
with 200 likes and no saves.

> Source: Andrei Zinkevich, April 17, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/azinkevich_here-are-8-pillars-of-the-full-funnel-abm-activity-7450875788679139336-QTuW
> 8 pillars of full-funnel ABM strategy, published in full
> with no paywall. 53 likes, 13 comments, 5 reposts — saved
> disproportionately because it functions as reference
> material, not a hot take.

---

### 1.7 Drive Every Post To One Destination

Every post should have one clear next step. Not three. One.

For 100Hires, the options are:
- A free hiring benchmark tool or audit
- A newsletter on hiring trends for SMBs
- A case study showing specific time-to-hire improvements

Pick one. Use it consistently for at least 90 days before
evaluating whether it converts.

> Source: Justin Welsh, April 21, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_great-lives-are-remarkably-simple-if-you-activity-7452322341453320192-ZoAR
> Every single post ends with the same newsletter CTA.
> 180,000 subscribers built from one consistent call to
> action, repeated without variation for years. The
> destination never changes. Only the content does.

## Section 2 — Where The Experts Disagree

### Disagreement 1: Brevity vs. Depth

**Katelyn Bourgoin** argues for extreme compression —
her best-performing posts are under 50 words. Her thesis:
clarity is the product. If you can't say it short, you
don't understand it well enough.

> Source: Katelyn Bourgoin, April 17, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/katebour_marketing-tactics-channels-and-tech-change-activity-7450863592847011841-TsXi
> "Marketing tactics, channels, and tech change rapidly.
> People don't." — 18 words. 140 likes. 64 comments.

**Andrei Zinkevich** publishes posts of 800+ words with
numbered frameworks, detailed sub-sections, and full
methodology breakdowns. His engagement rivals Bourgoin's
despite — or because of — the depth.

> Source: Andrei Zinkevich, April 15, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/azinkevich_this-simple-abm-playbook-helped-us-to-book-activity-7450143423220641793-mntB
> 800+ word ABM playbook. 70 likes, 34 comments, 5 reposts.

**My position:** Both are right for different objectives.
Bourgoin's compression works because she's building a broad
audience around a philosophical idea anyone can hold.
Zinkevich's depth works because he's pre-qualifying buyers
who are already considering a complex purchasing decision.

For 100Hires, I'd default to Zinkevich's model. The ICP —
HR managers and founders evaluating a tool that will change
their hiring process — responds better to demonstrated
expertise than to memorable one-liners. Short posts build
awareness. Long frameworks build the trust that closes deals.

---

### Disagreement 2: Personal Vulnerability vs. Pure Expertise

**Thibaut Souyris** regularly publishes posts about personal
anxiety, business pressure, and the emotional cost of growth.
His most engaged post in this research was about feeling
overwhelmed — not about sales tactics.

> Source: Thibaut Souyris, April 21, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/thibautsouyris_ive-been-feeling-a-weight-on-my-shoulders-activity-7452XXX
> "I've been feeling a weight on my shoulders for the past
> few weeks." — 33 likes, 25 comments. More engagement than
> his tactical content.

**Richard van der Blom** never publishes personal content.
Every post is data, research, or tactical breakdown.
Zero vulnerability. Maximum authority. Nearly 200,000
followers built entirely on expertise signals.

> Source: Richard van der Blom, April 20, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richardvanderblom_our-annual-algorithm-report-2025-coming-activity-7308091046624702464-WmxY

**My position:** Van der Blom's approach is the right model
for a B2B SaaS brand account. Personal vulnerability works
for individual creators building parasocial trust with a
broad audience. For a company account, it reads as
unfocused and dilutes the expertise signal that drives
purchasing decisions.

100Hires should leave vulnerability to its founders'
personal profiles — if they choose to use them. The company
account should be authoritative, specific, and relentlessly
useful.

### Disagreement 3: Posting Frequency

**Justin Welsh** posts every single day without exception
and has done so for years. His argument, implicit in his
entire presence: consistency compounds. Volume builds the
habit that eventually becomes an audience.

> Source: Justin Welsh, April 19, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/justinwelsh_im-convinced-most-people-are-performative-activity-7451597563285147648-2hjE
> Daily post. 3,333 likes. 861 comments. 138 reposts.

**Richard van der Blom's data** directly contradicts this:
his 2025 research across 1.8M posts shows 1-3 posts per
week as the optimal cadence, with over-posting leading to
measurable diminishing returns for most accounts.

> Source: Richard van der Blom, Algorithm Insights 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/richardvanderblom_our-annual-algorithm-report-2025-coming-activity-7308091046624702464-WmxY

**My position:** Van der Blom's data wins over Welsh's
anecdote — with one important caveat. Welsh is a solo
operator whose entire business model runs on personal
brand at scale. Daily posting makes sense for him because
presence is his product. For a SaaS company with a
marketing team handling multiple channels simultaneously,
maintaining quality control across daily output is
operationally unrealistic. 2-3 exceptional posts per week
will outperform 7 average ones every time.

## Section 3 — What I Rejected And Why

### Rejected: The "Comment To Receive" Tactic

Asking people to comment a word in exchange for a resource
sent via DM is a short-term reach hack that generates zero
pipeline value. The people who comment are chasing the free
resource, not interested in the product. The engagement is
algorithmically real but commercially hollow.

> Source: Sara Stella Lattanzio, April 14, 2025
> https://www.linkedin.com/posts/saralattanzio_bulletproof-tip-for-standing-out-on-linkedin-activity-7449432962472976384-mSoG
> "The vast majority of people playing this game never send
> anything and pocket the virality. Some steal other
> creators' content wholesale, just to keep the
> comment-for-comment engine running."

For 100Hires, this tactic would be actively damaging. HR
managers evaluating a serious purchasing decision would
see through it immediately. One post like this would
undermine months of credibility building. The short-term
reach gain is not worth the long-term authority cost.

---

### Rejected: Cross-Platform Repurposing As Primary Strategy

Justin Welsh's model of publishing simultaneously on
LinkedIn and X, using each platform's engagement as social
proof on the other, is intelligent for a solo creator with
an established audience on both platforms.

For an early-stage B2B SaaS company with limited resources,
it is a distraction. Building two audiences simultaneously
splits focus and dilutes content production capacity.
LinkedIn is where 100Hires' buyers actually are. X is not.

The resource investment required to maintain quality across
both platforms is not justified until the LinkedIn presence
is already generating measurable pipeline. Solving one
channel completely is worth more than half-solving two.

## Section 4 — My Original Idea

### The Product-As-Content Model: Show The Hiring
Process Live

Every expert in this research teaches LinkedIn strategy
for companies that sell to professionals. None of them
addresses the specific opportunity available to a company
whose product *is* the hiring process.

100Hires has an asset no competitor has: the ability to
demonstrate its product value by using it publicly.

**The idea:** run 100Hires' own hiring process entirely
on LinkedIn.

When 100Hires needs to hire — for any role — document
the entire process in public. Post the job description
with the exact screening criteria used in the platform.
Share the number of applications received in 48 hours.
Show the automated scoring in action. Publish the
interview structure. Announce the hire with the full
timeline and the key data points.

Every post in this sequence does three things
simultaneously:
1. Demonstrates the product working in real conditions
2. Attracts candidates — a secondary but genuine benefit
3. Shows HR managers exactly what their own hiring
   process could look like if they used the tool

This is not a case study. Case studies can be fabricated,
selectively edited, and written months after the fact.
Live documentation cannot. The credibility gap between
the two is enormous.

No competitor can replicate this because no competitor
has a product worth documenting in this way. 100Hires does.

### How to implement it — minimum viable version

1. Next time 100Hires opens a role, announce it as a
   LinkedIn post with the exact screening criteria used
   in the platform. Show the setup, not just the outcome.

2. At 48 hours, post an update: number of applicants,
   score distribution, time saved vs. manual screening.
   Use real numbers. Round numbers signal fabrication.

3. At close, post the outcome: role filled, total time
   from post to offer, one data point about the winning
   candidate's profile, and what the process would have
   looked like without automation.

Three posts. One hiring cycle. Undeniable proof of concept.
If the results are good, it becomes a repeatable content
format. If they're mixed, the transparency itself builds
more trust than a polished case study ever would.

## Section 5 — Weak Points Of This Playbook

**1. It assumes a consistent publishing cadence is
achievable.**
Everything in this playbook depends on publishing 2-3
quality posts per week without interruption. For a lean
team managing product, sales, and customer success
simultaneously, this is a real operational constraint
that this playbook does not solve. A content calendar
and a dedicated owner are prerequisites, not suggestions.

**2. The original data recommendation requires
infrastructure that may not exist.**
Recommending that 100Hires publish benchmark data from
its customer base assumes that data is being actively
collected, is statistically significant, and that
customers have consented to its aggregated use. None of
that is guaranteed. Publishing data that turns out to be
unrepresentative would cause more damage than not
publishing it at all.

**3. The ICP assumption may be too narrow.**
This playbook is built around HR managers and founders
at SMBs. But 100Hires also serves recruiting agencies
and operations leads whose priorities and content
consumption habits are different. A message that
resonates with an HR manager at a 50-person company
may not land with a recruiter managing 200 open roles
at an agency. This playbook does not address that
segmentation and should not be applied uniformly
across both audiences.

**4. This research contains no API-collected data or
YouTube transcripts.**
Posts were collected manually from LinkedIn screenshots.
The original research brief requested YouTube transcripts
via Supadata and API-based collection. That layer is
absent, which means video content strategies, podcast
insights, and longer-form thinking from these
practitioners are not reflected here. The playbook is
built on text posts only — a real limitation given that
video is currently the highest-reach format on LinkedIn.

**5. 30 posts is a small sample.**
Three posts per expert is enough to identify surface
patterns but not enough to draw statistically reliable
conclusions about what consistently performs. Some of
the posts collected may be outliers — unusually high
or low engagement relative to the author's typical
range. Conclusions should be treated as directional,
not definitive.

## Section 6 — Who I Would Not Recommend Following

### Justin Welsh

Welsh is the most followed name on this list and the
least useful source for a B2B SaaS growth marketer
building from scratch.

His content generates extraordinary engagement —
3,000 to 4,000 likes per post — because it operates
at the level of universal human psychology: self-
confidence, authenticity, freedom from approval-seeking.
These themes resonate with anyone who has ever felt
stuck in their career or life. The emotional mechanics
are real and the writing is genuinely good.

But none of it has anything to do with B2B SaaS. There
is no hiring insight, no product positioning thinking,
no GTM framework. The engagement is real. The
applicability to 100Hires is effectively zero.

More importantly, his model is structurally unreplicable
without two things most companies don't have: years of
daily publishing and a pre-existing audience built from
a successful SaaS exit that gave him credibility before
he wrote a single post. Following his framework without
his starting conditions produces philosophical content
that nobody reads — because the trust that makes his
content land was compounded over years, not manufactured
in weeks.

Welsh belongs in this research as a reference point for
what scale looks like at the top of the market, and as
a case study in the long-game economics of personal
brand building. He does not belong in the execution
layer of this playbook.

## Summary: The 5 Things That Actually Matter

If 100Hires implements nothing else from this playbook,
these five actions will move the needle:

1. **Own one narrative.** Pick the specific tension you
   solve — hiring is broken because it's still manual —
   and return to it from every angle, every week.

2. **Publish original data.** One data post per month
   built from real customer outcomes. Time-to-hire,
   screening hours saved, response rates. Numbers that
   only you have.

3. **Post 2-3 times per week.** Never daily. Never
   sporadic. Consistency over 12 months beats intensity
   over 3.

4. **Engage before and after every post.** 15-30 minutes
   of genuine commenting on relevant content is not
   optional — it is part of the distribution system.
   Treat it as such.

5. **Drive to one destination.** Pick a single CTA and
   use it without variation for 90 days before changing
   anything. Changing it every week means you will never
   know what works.

Everything else is optimization.
These five are the foundation.
