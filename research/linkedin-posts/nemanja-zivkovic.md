## Context: Who is Nemanja Zivkovic?

Zivkovic is the founder of Funky Marketing, a B2B tech and SaaS 
growth strategy firm based in Serbia, specializing in high ACV 
and long sales cycles. His positioning is deliberately contrarian: 
"I don't do marketing. I build commercial systems that compound 
revenue." He has spent 7+ years building a LinkedIn presence 
that operates as both a demand generation engine and a hiring 
funnel, and he documents the process with unusual transparency. 
His podcast has 130+ episodes, each featuring practitioners 
rather than theorists.

What makes him valuable to this research is his insistence on 
the long game. He doesn't optimize for engagement — he optimizes 
for recognition from the right people over time. His content 
strategy is essentially a test of conviction: stay in the same 
voice, for long enough, until the audience catches up.

15% explicitly mention "full-stack marketer" as the new

One more quote from the same research: "A product marketer

document. Before, a marketer would never be expected to set 
up an API."

Full-stack marketer, a phrase that for years sounded like a 
consultant buzzword nobody could define precisely or explain 
in practice, is becoming a concrete job description being 
quietly rewritten across hundreds of marketing teams.

Here's why this changes the math for anyone building a career 
in B2B marketing 👇

A company that has one well-equipped full-stack marketer, who 
can write copy, build a landing page, set up automation, analyze 
data, create a competitor report, and connect an API without 
waiting for a developer sprint, doesn't need the same number 
of specialists as three years ago for the same output.

This isn't hypothetical. It's what's happening on the teams in 
the 7/7 AI skill rating category in the Wynter data.

Two perspectives that are both true 👆

This is bad news for marketers who think being excellent at one 
discipline is enough and who are waiting for things to "settle 
down." The job market isn't waiting.

This is great news for marketers actively developing outside 
their core. Because every skill you add creates a competitive 
gap against those who aren't adding anything - and that gap 
compounds.

The traditional model: a content specialist, an analytics 
specialist, a paid specialist, an SEO specialist. Everyone 
does their thing, handoffs between disciplines, coordination 
that costs time.

The new model builders are constructing: a smaller team, broader 
competencies per person, AI filling the gaps in specialization.

One well-equipped full-stack marketer can cover a workflow that 
previously required 2–3 people - not equally deep in every 
discipline, but good enough for 80% of cases.

For companies in scale-up mode, this directly changes how hiring 
planning and team design should look in the marketing function.

In 1991, Chris Barron wrote "Two Princes", one of my favorite

He says the song doesn't have a chorus.

He wrote it at 19. Busboy in a Princeton diner, angry that the

Went home, picked up a guitar, wrote the first line on a yellow

The "just go ahead now" hook?
That's how his friend Mickett actually talked.
He wrote down the way his friends already spoke to each other.

The most-sung line in the song wasn't written. It was transcribed.

And the whole song rides one groove. No chorus. No big lift. No 
"here comes the hook" moment. No switch into performance mode.

That's the trick.

One more thing. He wasn't trying to write a hit.

"Two Princes" was one of hundreds of songs he'd written by 19 — 
shows played, bands formed, reps put in. The hit was a byproduct 
of volume in the same voice.

Then the album came out in August 1991 and sold 60,000 copies. 
Mostly ignored for a year. "Two Princes" wasn't released as a 
single until 1993 - 18 months later.

Then it went five-times platinum! Boom!

He didn't change the song.
He just kept showing up in the same voice until the world caught 
up.

And here's the part nobody talks about: the same song is on VH1's 
"100 Greatest Songs of the '90s" (at 41) AND Blender's "50 Worst 
Songs Ever" list (at 21).

Same song. Same voice.

Half the world loves it, half hates it.

That's what authentic work does.
If everyone likes it, you sanded off the thing that made it work.

Most people on LinkedIn think consistency kills authenticity 
because they run two modes - the polished post and the real them.

Then they oscillate between the two and burn out in six months. 
Stupid!

The people who stay consistent AND authentic don't have two

same person.

They put in the reps before anyone's watching.
They keep showing up in that voice before anyone's clapping.

And they accept that the same voice earning them fans is earning 
them critics - at the same time.

You don't get there by performing harder. You get there by 
narrowing the gap between how you talk and how you write until 
there's nothing left to bridge.

No chorus.
Just the song.

## Key Patterns Observed

- **Storytelling as the primary argument format:** Post 3 makes 
  a sophisticated point about authentic content strategy — and 
  does it entirely through the story of a 1991 pop song. Not a 
  framework, not a list, not data. A story that ends with the 
  argument embedded in it. This is the highest-difficulty content 
  format on LinkedIn because it requires actual narrative skill, 
  which means almost nobody does it well. When it works, it 
  generates the kind of comments that say "damn, that's a post" 
  — which is exactly what his own first comment says.

- **Bilingual publishing as a signal of dual market strategy:** 
  Publishing the same core idea in Serbian and English on the 
  same day is not an accident. It suggests he is building two 
  distinct audiences simultaneously — the regional market where 
  he has brand authority, and the international B2B SaaS market 
  he wants to penetrate. For B2B SaaS companies with multiple 
  target markets, this is an underused distribution strategy.

- **Third-party research as credibility anchor:** Post 2 opens 
  with a direct quote from Wynter research (N=100) before making 
  any argument of his own. This is the same pattern as van der 
  Blom and Greenberg: lead with someone else's data, then layer 
  your interpretation on top. It signals intellectual honesty 
  — "I'm not making this up, here's the evidence" — and makes 
  the post much harder to dismiss than one built on personal 
  opinion alone.
