## Context: Who is Katelyn Bourgoin?

Bourgoin is the founder of Customer Camp and creator of the Why We 
Buy newsletter, focused on buyer psychology for B2B marketers and 
founders. Her core thesis is that tactics and channels are 
commodities — the durable competitive advantage in marketing is 
understanding why people actually make decisions. With 65,000+ 
newsletter subscribers and consistent top engagement on LinkedIn, 
she has built one of the most concentrated audiences in the B2B 
marketing space: people who take messaging and positioning seriously.

For this research, she represents a dimension most LinkedIn content 
strategy guides ignore entirely: the psychological architecture 
underneath effective content. Understanding what makes a message 
stick, what triggers a save, what compels a comment — this is 
Bourgoin's territory, and it's foundational for any B2B SaaS 
company trying to build organic presence that actually converts.

## Key Patterns Observed

- **Maximum compression, maximum engagement:** Bourgoin's three 
  posts combined contain fewer words than a single post from most 
  other experts on this list. Yet her engagement is among the 
  highest — Post 2 generated 150 likes and 70 comments with 
  under 60 words. This is a deliberate strategy: compression forces 
  clarity, and clarity is what makes content shareable. For B2B 
  SaaS like 100Hires, the lesson is that the instinct to over-explain 
  a product's value is the enemy of engagement.

- **Owning a concept, not a topic:** Every post reinforces the same 
  core idea — "mental real estate," owning an idea in the buyer's 
  mind. She doesn't post about marketing broadly. She posts about 
  one thing from every possible angle. This is the most durable 
  LinkedIn content strategy because the algorithm rewards topical 
  consistency, and audiences remember people who stand for something 
  specific.

- **Tension as the engagement engine:** Post 3 is 18 words. It 
  generates 140 likes because it creates cognitive tension — tactics 
  change, people don't. The reader is forced to pause and process. 
  This is applied buyer psychology: she uses the same principles she 
  teaches to make her own content work. Watching what she does is 
  as instructive as reading what she says.
